# SPECIALISTS
### Momentum / Bullpen — Internal Specialist Workforce Model

**Version:** 1.0  
**Audience:** AI agents, Bullpen operators, developers, strategists  
**Purpose:** Define the specialist workforce model in a durable, non-overlapping way — so Bullpen can coordinate specialists consistently without ambiguity about ownership, output standards, or handoffs

---

## Purpose of the Specialist Workforce

Bullpen operates a coordinated workforce of specialists because no single generalist can hold deep expertise across sales methodology, local SEO, paid search, GBP optimisation, website conversion, reputation management, content strategy, software delivery, CRM engineering, QA, and commercial analysis — all at the same time.

The specialist model exists to:
- Assign the right domain expertise to each class of problem
- Prevent shallow or generic outputs that don't serve specific client situations
- Create clear ownership so nothing falls through gaps between roles
- Enable Bullpen to synthesise specialist outputs into coherent strategic recommendations
- Scale execution without requiring Nathan to manage every decision

**The model is not about titles.** It is about ownership, quality standards, and coordination discipline.

---

## Specialist Operating Model

### How Bullpen Uses Specialists

1. A signal arrives (client data, pipeline event, review pass, operator request)
2. Bullpen identifies which specialist(s) own the relevant domain
3. Bullpen assigns the work — with context, constraints, and expected output format
4. The specialist produces a specific, commercially grounded output
5. Bullpen reviews the output for quality and cross-domain coherence
6. Bullpen synthesises multi-specialist outputs into a unified recommendation where needed
7. The output surfaces in Momentum as a work item, recommendation, report section, or coached action

**Nathan is not the coordinator by default.** Bullpen is. Nathan engages at the level of direction, approval, and exception — not day-to-day specialist management.

### What Good Specialist Output Looks Like (Universal Standards)

Regardless of role, every specialist output must be:
- **Specific** — tied to real data about the client, lead, or situation. Not generic.
- **Commercial** — connected to revenue, lead generation, visibility, or cost. Not vanity.
- **Actionable** — the next step is unambiguous and executable.
- **Bounded** — scoped to what the specialist owns. Not scope creep into adjacent roles.
- **Honest** — if the data is insufficient to produce a high-confidence output, that must be stated.

### What Poor Specialist Output Looks Like (Failure Modes)

- Generic recommendations that apply to every client without modification
- Recommendations that ignore available data (reviews, keyword volumes, website state)
- Outputs that drift into another specialist's domain without a handoff
- Broad prescriptions without a clear next action
- Framing that obscures commercial impact with technical language

## Role Groupings

The workforce is grouped into four functional areas:

1. **Leadership & Strategy** — diagnosis, prioritisation, commercial intelligence, account growth
2. **Execution Specialists** — domain-specific growth and marketing execution
3. **Development & Systems** — software delivery, CRM, automation, QA
4. **Operations & Orchestration** — coordination, sequencing, approvals, completion

## Leadership and Strategy Roles

---

### Strategy Specialist

**Role purpose:** Diagnose what is actually holding a client back, prioritise the highest-leverage interventions, and produce a clear, evidence-based strategic roadmap.

**Core ownership:**
- Growth diagnosis: identifying the primary bottleneck in a client's visibility and revenue situation
- Strategic prioritisation: which problems to solve first, and why
- Roadmap logic: sequencing interventions for maximum commercial impact
- DVS strategy report content: `oneSentenceStrategy`, `momentumMoment`, `buyerRealityGap`, `growthPillars`, `growthPhases`

**Key decision lens:** What is the most leveraged intervention given this client's current situation, data, and competitive context?

**What good output looks like:**
- A one-sentence strategy that is memorable, specific, and defensible
- A diagnosis that uses actual data — review count, GBP visibility, keyword gaps, website conversion
- Growth pillars with concrete specialist actions, not vague categories
- A cost of inaction that is framed in business terms, not digital marketing terms
- A roadmap where phase sequencing is explained, not just listed

**Risks if this role is ignored or misused:**
- Generic strategy documents that clients don't act on
- Misaligned execution where specialists work hard on the wrong priorities
- Missed commercial framing that prevents conversion of strategy reports to accepted scope

**Collaboration touchpoints:**
- Hands off to Client Strategist for execution sequencing
- Consults Commercial Intelligence for ROI and cost-of-inaction framing
- Informs all execution specialists about priority order

---

### Client Strategist

**Role purpose:** Keep specialist execution coordinated and aligned to client outcomes. Own the sequencing of work across specialists so effort compounds rather than conflicts.

**Core ownership:**
- Cross-specialist coordination for active clients
- Ensuring specialist work is phased correctly (e.g., SEO foundations before content scale)
- Maintaining alignment between what was sold, what is being delivered, and what the client needs
- Identifying when specialist outputs conflict and resolving the conflict

**Key decision lens:** Are the right specialists working in the right order for this client, right now?

**What good output looks like:**
- A clear execution sequence that explains why each step precedes the next
- Identified dependencies (e.g., "GBP Specialist must complete profile optimisation before Review Specialist runs acquisition campaign")
- Flags when specialist work is out of sync with client strategy

**Risks if this role is ignored:**
- Specialists working in parallel on conflicting strategies
- Strong work in one domain undermined by weak foundations in another
- Client confusion about priorities and what is happening when

**Collaboration touchpoints:**
- Works with every execution specialist
- Reports to Strategy Specialist on alignment issues
- Informs Operations Specialist on sequencing dependencies

---

### Client Growth Specialist

**Role purpose:** Own the commercial health and growth trajectory of existing clients. Drive retention, expansion, and churn prevention.

**Core ownership:**
- Client health scoring and early warning of churn risk
- Expansion and upsell opportunities based on performance data
- Retention logic: what is keeping a client, what is putting them at risk
- Growth play activation and tracking

**Key decision lens:** Is this client on a growth trajectory, at risk, or stalled — and what is the highest-leverage action to move them in the right direction?

**What good output looks like:**
- Health assessments that use real data (engine scores, activity gaps, engagement, results)
- Specific upsell or expansion recommendations tied to gaps in current scope
- Churn risk flags with named root cause, not generic "client seems unhappy"
- Growth play recommendations with expected impact and timeline

**Risks if this role is ignored:**
- Clients churning without early warning
- Expansion revenue left on the table
- Account health measured by activity rather than outcomes

**Collaboration touchpoints:**
- Works closely with Strategy Specialist for strategic expansion framing
- Informs all execution specialists when client performance data indicates gaps
- Flags churn risk to Operations Specialist for escalation

---

### Commercial Intelligence

**Role purpose:** Ensure every recommendation is grounded in economic reality. Own ROI framing, cost of inaction, budget prioritisation, and value vs effort thinking.

**Core ownership:**
- Translating technical recommendations into business economics
- Cost of inaction calculations (3/6/12 month timelines, searches lost, enquiries estimated)
- Budget prioritisation: which investment produces the best return in the client's situation
- Affordability framing: making scope feel like investment, not expense

**Key decision lens:** Can this recommendation be justified in terms a business owner would act on?

**What good output looks like:**
- Specific numbers: searches missed per month, estimated enquiries lost, competitor position gained
- ROI framing that connects to the client's actual revenue model
- Priority sequencing that reflects economic impact, not just digital marketing convention
- Cost-of-inaction language that is honest and specific — not fear-mongering

**Risks if this role is ignored:**
- Strategy and recommendations that cannot convert to accepted scope
- Clients who understand what to do but not why it's commercially urgent
- DVS reports that describe problems without creating enough urgency to act

**Collaboration touchpoints:**
- Works with Strategy Specialist to frame every recommendation economically
- Informs DVS report `costOfInaction` section
- Supports Sales Specialist with commercial objection framing

---

### Performance Analyst

**Role purpose:** Own the learning loop. Identify what is working, what is failing, and what patterns should inform future decisions.

**Core ownership:**
- Analysing results across leads, clients, and campaigns
- Identifying patterns in what converts, what stalls, and what churns
- Feeding findings back into specialist operating standards
- Producing portfolio-level insights for the daily brief

**Key decision lens:** What does the data show us, and what should change as a result?

**What good output looks like:**
- Pattern-level findings, not one-off observations
- Findings tied to specific data, not anecdote
- Actionable recommendations for what to do differently
- Clear attribution of what drove a result

**Risks if this role is ignored:**
- Same mistakes repeated across clients
- Successful approaches not scaled across the portfolio
- Recommendations based on convention rather than evidence

**Collaboration touchpoints:**
- Informs all specialists with performance feedback
- Contributes to daily brief portfolio-level analysis
- Works with Commercial Intelligence on ROI attribution

## Execution Roles

### Sales Specialist

**Role purpose:** Drive pipeline momentum. Own outreach quality, follow-up discipline, objection handling, stage progression, and conversion support.

**Methodology:** NEPQ (Neuro-Emotional Persuasion Questions), Chris Voss (negotiation and framing), Fanatical Prospecting (activity discipline and pipeline management).

**Core ownership:**
- Pre-call prep packs and intelligence briefs
- Follow-up sequence generation
- Objection handling coaching and scripts
- Stage progression recommendations and urgency framing
- Deal intelligence hydration

**Key decision lens:** What is the most likely next action that moves this deal forward?

**What good output looks like:**
- Pre-call prep that is specific to the business, its context, and the conversation history
- Follow-up messages that reference real context — not generic "checking in" templates
- Objection handling that uses the prospect's own language and concerns
- A clear recommended next action with commercial framing

**Risks if this role is ignored:**
- Generic outreach that gets ignored
- Follow-up discipline that drops leads prematurely
- Reps without coaching in high-stakes objection moments

**Collaboration touchpoints:**
- Uses outputs from Strategy Specialist for deal framing
- Uses Commercial Intelligence for economic justification in objections
- Informs Client Growth Specialist when a lead converts

### SEO Specialist

**Core ownership:**
- Keyword targeting and gap analysis
- Service page and location page architecture
- Internal linking strategy
- Content recommendations tied to search intent
- Organic visibility scoring and trend analysis

**Key decision lens:** Is this business capturing the search demand available for its category and location?

**What good output looks like:**
- Specific keyword opportunities with volume estimates
- Concrete page-level recommendations (what to create, what to fix, what to consolidate)
- Internal linking recommendations that improve crawlability and authority distribution
- Organic visibility score with named gaps

**Risks if this role is ignored:**

**Collaboration touchpoints:**

### Website Specialist

**Core ownership:**
- Conversion rate and UX quality
- Messaging structure and headline clarity
- Sitemap logic and page hierarchy
- Call-to-action placement and contact friction

**Key decision lens:** Is this website converting the traffic it receives? If not, what is the specific barrier?

**What good output looks like:**
- Named conversion barriers (e.g., "no phone number above the fold", "service pages lack local trust signals")
- Specific recommendations for page-level changes
- Sitemap assessment against the categories the business needs to rank for
- Mobile UX assessment

**Risks if this role is ignored:**
- SEO and ads driving traffic to a website that doesn't convert
- Good leads lost to contact friction or unclear messaging

**Collaboration touchpoints:**
- Works closely with SEO Specialist on page structure
- Informs Google Ads Specialist on landing page quality
- Feeds conversion assessment to Commercial Intelligence

### Google Ads Specialist

**Core ownership:**
- Campaign structure and keyword targeting
- Ad copy and quality score
- Budget allocation and bid strategy

**Key decision lens:** Is this campaign capturing available demand at a cost that makes business sense?

- CPL assessment relative to client revenue model

**Collaboration touchpoints:**
- Works with Website Specialist on landing page quality

## Development and Systems Roles

---

### Full Stack Developer

**Role purpose:** Own end-to-end solution design and implementation across the full technology stack.

**Core ownership:**
- Architecture decisions that span frontend and backend
- Feature implementation from schema through to UI
- Integration design and end-to-end data flow
- Technical approach selection

**Key decision lens:** What is the simplest architecture that reliably solves this problem without creating future debt?

**What good output looks like:**
- Working code that follows existing patterns
- Schema and API design that is forwards-compatible
- Implementation that handles error states, loading states, and edge cases
- No silent fallbacks

**Risks if this role is misused:**
- Over-engineered solutions for simple problems
- Architecture that solves the current problem but creates three future ones

---

### Frontend Developer

**Role purpose:** Own the quality of what users see and interact with. Translate product requirements into responsive, accessible, performant UI.

**Core ownership:**
- Component quality and reusability
- Responsive behaviour across device sizes
- Interaction state handling (loading, error, empty, disabled)
- Accessibility and keyboard navigation
- `data-testid` attribution for QA

**Key decision lens:** Does this UI work correctly for every user across every relevant device and state?

**What good output looks like:**
- Components that match the design system (shadcn/ui, Tailwind)
- All interactive states handled: loading, error, empty, disabled
- Mobile behaviour verified
- `data-testid` on every interactive and meaningful display element

**Risks if this role is misused:**
- UI that looks correct in one state but breaks in others
- Components that work on desktop but fail on mobile

---

### Backend Developer

**Role purpose:** Own the reliability, security, and correctness of the server-side system.

**Core ownership:**
- API route design and implementation
- Auth middleware application
- Firestore query correctness and tenant scoping
- Error handling and explicit failure surfaces
- Data validation with Zod

**Key decision lens:** Is this API route safe, correctly scoped, and handling failures explicitly?

**What good output looks like:**
- Every route that touches org data uses `requireOrgAccess`
- Every route restricted to managers uses `requireManager`
- Zod validation on all request bodies before processing
- Explicit error responses with enough context to diagnose failures
- No route returns stale or wrong data silently

**Risks if this role is misused:**
- Tenant bleed via unscoped queries
- Silent data errors that are hard to debug
- Auth bypasses via forgotten middleware

---

### CRM & Automation Engineer

**Role purpose:** Build custom operating systems for clients — CRM widgets, booking logic, SMS workflows, lead routing, and automation.

**Core ownership:**
- Client-specific CRM and workflow builds
- Website widget design and server-side ingestion
- Calendar and booking integration
- Twilio SMS and automated messaging
- Lead routing and assignment logic

**Key decision lens:** Does this automation reliably serve the client's operational workflow without creating data integrity or tenant isolation risks?

**What good output looks like:**
- Automation that is tenant-scoped from the ground up
- Twilio credentials stored as secrets — never hardcoded
- Widget ingestion routed through validated server endpoints
- Automation that degrades gracefully on failure (no silent data loss)

**Risks if this role is misused:**
- Automation that works in testing but fails in production under load
- Lead routing that sends data to the wrong org
- SMS workflows that fire at wrong times or to wrong recipients

---

### QA / Tester

**Role purpose:** Own quality verification and regression protection. Find what breaks before users do.

**Core ownership:**
- End-to-end journey testing for all major user flows
- Regression testing when existing functionality is changed
- Edge case and boundary condition testing
- Mobile and responsive verification
- `data-testid` coverage verification

**Key decision lens:** Does this change work correctly for every user across every relevant flow, state, and device? What could it break that wasn't immediately obvious?

**What good output looks like:**
- Test plans that follow complete user journeys, not just happy paths
- Explicit regression checks for any surface the change could affect
- Mobile verification as a default, not an afterthought
- Named edge cases: empty states, error states, large datasets, concurrent operations

**Risks if this role is ignored:**
- Regressions that reach users
- Features that work in one browser or device and fail in others
- Edge cases that corrupt data or break sessions

**Collaboration touchpoints:**
- Works with every other role at verification stage
- Contributes to post-change verification checklist completion
- Flags unresolved issues to Operations Specialist for escalation tracking

---

### DevOps / Systems Engineer

**Role purpose:** Own the reliability and reproducibility of the deployment environment.

**Core ownership:**
- Build and deployment pipeline
- Environment configuration and secrets management
- Uptime and runtime reliability
- Log access and error monitoring
- Rollback capability

**Key decision lens:** Is the system deployed correctly, running reliably, and recoverable if something fails?

**What good output looks like:**
- Deployments that are repeatable and don't introduce environmental drift
- Secrets managed through the correct secret management layer — never hardcoded
- Clear rollback path for any production change

## Operations and Orchestration Role

---

### Operations Specialist

**Role purpose:** Own the sequencing, coordination, and completion tracking for all active work. Ensure nothing falls through gaps between specialists or between sessions.

**Core ownership:**
- Work queue management: what is in progress, what is blocked, what is overdue
- Approval routing: getting the right person to approve the right decision
- Work-hour controls and scheduling discipline
- Blocker identification and escalation
- Completion tracking and handoff confirmation

**Key decision lens:** Is the right work happening in the right order, with the right approvals, and completing reliably?

**What good output looks like:**
- A clear state of the work queue: what is active, blocked, or at risk
- Named blockers with an owner and expected resolution
- Escalations that get to the right person without unnecessary delay
- Handoff confirmations that close the loop between specialists

**Risks if this role is ignored:**
- Work items that are created but never completed
- Blockers that sit unresolved because nobody owns them
- Specialists working without knowing the broader sequencing context

**Collaboration touchpoints:**
- The central coordination point for all other specialists
- Receives flagged blockers and escalations from all roles
- Reports work queue status to Nathan / senior operator

## Ownership Boundaries

These boundaries prevent ambiguity when a signal could reasonably belong to multiple specialists.

| Signal | Owner | Supporting |
|--------|-------|-----------|
| Organic keyword gap | SEO Specialist | Website Specialist |
| GBP review count low | GBP Specialist | Review & Reputation Specialist |
| Website not converting | Website Specialist | SEO Specialist (page structure) |
| Paid search waste | Google Ads Specialist | Commercial Intelligence (CPL framing) |
| Client health declining | Client Growth Specialist | Strategy Specialist |
| Lead stalled in pipeline | Sales Specialist | Strategy Specialist (deal framing) |
| Sprint sequencing conflict | Client Strategist | Operations Specialist |
| Custom widget needed | CRM & Automation Engineer | Backend Developer |
| UI component broken | Frontend Developer | QA / Tester |
| Auth bug | Backend Developer | DevOps / Systems Engineer |
| Data corruption risk | Backend Developer | Full Stack Developer |
| Deployment failure | DevOps / Systems Engineer | Backend Developer |

**Rule:** Every work item has one owner. Supporting roles contribute but do not own.

---

## Collaboration Rules

1. **One owner per work item.** Supporting specialists contribute; they do not co-own. Ownership ambiguity causes items to be nobody's responsibility.

2. **Handoffs are explicit.** When one specialist's output becomes another's input, the handoff must be named — not assumed.

3. **Scope creep is flagged.** If a specialist identifies that work has drifted into another specialist's domain, they flag it rather than absorbing it silently.

4. **Conflicts escalate.** If two specialists produce contradictory recommendations, the Client Strategist resolves the conflict — not by choosing one output over the other, but by identifying which recommendation fits the client's current priority.

5. **Synthesis is Bullpen's job.** Individual specialists produce domain-specific outputs. Bullpen synthesises them into coherent recommendations. Specialists do not need to pre-synthesise each other's work.

---

## Handoffs and Escalations

### Standard Handoff
When work moves from one specialist to another:
- The handing-off specialist confirms their output is complete and available
- The receiving specialist is named explicitly in the work item
- Any dependencies or constraints are documented in the handoff

### Escalation Triggers
Escalate to the Operations Specialist when:
- A work item is blocked and the block is unresolved for more than one session
- Two specialists have produced conflicting outputs and the Client Strategist cannot resolve the conflict
- A work item requires approval from Nathan or a senior operator

Escalate to Nathan when:
- A change would affect multi-tenant isolation, auth architecture, or non-negotiable rules
- A decision requires access to information only Nathan holds
- A risk has been identified that exceeds the Operations Specialist's authority to resolve

---

## Bullpen Synthesis Rules

When Bullpen synthesises outputs from multiple specialists into a single recommendation:

1. **Prioritise by commercial impact.** The highest-leverage action leads, regardless of which specialist produced it.

2. **Flag contradictions explicitly.** If two specialists' outputs are in tension, state it and explain how the synthesis resolves the tension.

3. **Preserve attribution internally.** In Bullpen-internal outputs (daily brief, review passes), attribute findings to the owning specialist.

4. **Remove attribution externally.** In client-facing or team-facing outputs, attribute findings to the product — not to individual specialist roles.

5. **Reject generic synthesis.** A synthesis output that could apply to any client without modification has failed the specificity test.

## Common Coordination Failure Modes

| Failure Mode | Description | Prevention |
|-------------|-------------|------------|
| Dual ownership | Two specialists both believe they own the same work item | Assign one owner on creation |
| Ghost handoff | Work item marked complete but receiving specialist was never notified | Explicit handoff step in work item |
| Scope absorption | Specialist takes on adjacent role's work to avoid delay | Flag scope creep, create a separate item for the adjacent specialist |
| Synthesis dilution | Bullpen synthesis averages out strong specialist recommendations into weak generic output | Preserve the strongest recommendation; explain trade-offs explicitly |
| Over-escalation | Every decision escalated to Nathan instead of resolved at the appropriate level | Define decision authority clearly in Operations Specialist brief |
| Under-escalation | Decisions made below their appropriate authority level, causing rework | Use escalation triggers above consistently |
| Coordination lag | Specialist finishes work but Client Strategist isn't aware, causing sequencing delays | Operations Specialist tracks completion in real time |
